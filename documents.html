Theology Paper - Unapologetic

[2023]

To ensure embedded content renders properly, please load this webpage in a browser application like Firefox or Chrome

Although raised with a traditional Christian upbringing, I began questioning the foundations of my faith during postsecondary education. Within STEM there is an expectation that professionals should think and act rationally - my faith seemed to be in defiance of this principle, especially since I was unable to rationalise any specific reasons for my belief in God at that time.

In an attempt to justify the convictions of my personal faith, I began exploring various arguments for the existence of God. Reading the works of authors like Richard Swinburne, Alvin Plantinga, C. Stephen Evans, and Jerry Walls helped me begin to understand several of the tradtional arguments for the existence of God, and exposed me to the field of philsophy. The paper rendered below is my attempt to summarise several specific arguments which I found personally compelling.

Because the title of the paper is somewhat edgy, I will now elaborate upon it's significance in excessive rigor. Whenever I told a classmate or colleague that I was religious, I always felt as though a judgment was made upon my rationality, and that I should apologise for being silly. Even though I knew most of my classmates and colleagues would never judge each other for religious tendancies, I wanted to be confident enough in my ability to rationalise my faith that I would no longer feel the need to be apologetic about my convictions.
